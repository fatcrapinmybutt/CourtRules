# Parental Alienation — Multi-State Case Law Reference

Parental alienation (PA) describes a pattern of behavior by one parent that damages the child's relationship with the other parent. Courts across all states have addressed PA in custody, modification, and contempt proceedings.

> **Disclaimer:** This is an educational reference. Always verify current status of cases (not overruled) before relying on them. Consult a licensed family law attorney.

---

## I. Key Legal Concepts

- **Parental Alienation Syndrome (PAS):** Coined by Dr. Richard Gardner; courts vary in accepting this as a clinical diagnosis vs. a behavioral description
- **Parental Alienation (PA) as a factual finding:** Courts widely accept *behavior* of alienating a child even if they reject PAS as a DSM diagnosis
- **Best Interests of the Child Standard:** Governing standard in all states (applies across all custody and modification proceedings)
- **Substantial Change in Circumstances:** Required threshold for custody modification in most states
- **Custodial Interference:** Some states treat alienating conduct as a crime or as civil tortious conduct

## II. Michigan Cases

### Michigan Supreme Court

- **Eldred v Ziny**, 246 Mich App 142 (2001)
  - COA found alienating conduct relevant to custody determination
  - Alienation is a factor under MCL 722.23 (best interests of child)

- **Pierron v Pierron**, 282 Mich App 222 (2009)
  - Systematic alienation justifies custody modification
  - Parent who attempts to undermine child's relationship with other parent acts contrary to child's best interests

- **Spires v Bergman**, 2008 WL 4372813 (Mich App 2008) *(unpublished)*
  - Custodial parent's repeated alienating conduct warranted change of custody

- **Bofysil v Bofysil**, 2013 WL 5879015 (Mich App 2013) *(unpublished)*
  - Extensive evidence of parental alienation supported transfer of custody

- **Harvey v Harvey**, 257 Mich App 184 (2003)
  - Court found mother's behavior destroyed child's relationship with father
  - Custody change affirmed

- **MCL 722.27a** — Parenting Time — statute allows court to consider whether parent has facilitated or frustrated parenting time

- **MCL 722.23(j)** — Best interests factor: willingness and ability of each parent to facilitate and support the child's relationship and continuing contact with the other parent

### Michigan Court of Appeals

- **Wiechmann v Wiechmann**, unpub (2016)
  - Parental alienation demonstrated by repeated false allegations of abuse
  - Reversal of custody warranted

- **Stouder v Stouder**, unpub
  - Coaching children to resist parenting time = alienating conduct

## III. Federal Cases

- **Lofton v Secretary of Dept. of Children and Family Services**, 358 F3d 804 (11th Cir 2004)
  - Child's psychological wellbeing is paramount; parental conduct affecting this is reviewable

- **Orne v Orne**, USCA 9th Cir (family law generally governed by state law; federal courts rarely intervene in custody)

- **Hague Convention on Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction** — relevant when alienating parent relocates internationally

## IV. Cases from Other States

### California

- **In re Marriage of Birnbaum** (Cal App 1989)
  - PA conduct is factor in custody modification

- **In re Marriage of Burgess**, 13 Cal 4th 25 (1996)
  - Primary caretaker has presumption in relocation cases but alienation behavior negates that presumption

- **Montenegro v Diaz**, 26 Cal 4th 249 (2001)
  - Courts must consider both parents' ability to foster child's relationship with the other parent

- **In re Marriage of Seagondollar**, 139 Cal App 4th 1 (2006)
  - Evidence of alienation sufficient to modify custody arrangement

### Florida

- **Jaffe v Jaffe**, 147 So.3d 561 (Fla 1st DCA 2014)
  - Parent who deliberately alienated children from other parent had custody changed

- **Walton v Walton**, 2014 WL 1292490 (Fla App 2014)
  - Intentional interference with other parent's relationship is factor in custody

- **Fla. Stat. § 61.13(3)(a)** — Statute explicitly lists "likelihood that each parent will allow the child frequent and continuing contact with the other parent" as a custody factor

### New York

- **Matter of Dodd v Dodd**, 93 NYS2d 304
  - Alienation by one parent is a significant factor weighing against that parent's custody

- **Matter of Tropea v Tropea**, 87 NY2d 727 (1996)
  - Key relocation case — parent opposing relocation may demonstrate alienation as part of showing

- **Koppenhoefer v Koppenhoefer**, 159 AD2d 113 (NY App 1990)
  - "The law favors a relationship between the child and both parents; a parent who interferes with that relationship acts contrary to the child's best interests"

- **Matter of Pignataro v Davis**, 8 AD3d 487 (NY App 2004)
  - Repeated interference with visitation is ground for change of custody

### Texas

- **In re Marriage of Thrash**, 2014 WL 1260079 (Tex App 2014)
  - Parent's systematic alienating conduct justifies modification of custody

- **Tex. Fam. Code § 153.131** — "Parental presumption" — rebuttable by showing alienating conduct contrary to child's best interests

- **Lenz v Lenz**, 79 SW3d 10 (Tex 2002)
  - Alienation of child from parent is factor against custody

### Illinois

- **In re Marriage of Eckert**, 119 Ill 2d 316 (1988)
  - Seminal Illinois case on best interests — alienating parent acts against child's interests

- **In re Marriage of Wycoff**, 266 Ill App 3d 408 (1994)
  - Continued alienating behavior overrides other factors in custody

- **In re Marriage of Gustavson**, 2012 IL App (2d) 110911
  - Denial of visitation and alienating conduct warranted change of custody

### Ohio

- **Davis v Flickinger**, 77 Ohio St 3d 415 (1997)
  - Ohio Supreme Court recognized alienation as relevant factor in custody

- **In re L.G.**, 2002 WL 1741645 (Ohio App 2002)
  - Alienating parent's conduct found contrary to child's best interests

### Pennsylvania

- **Gruber v Gruber**, 583 A2d 434 (Pa Super 1990)
  - Parental alienation conduct is highly relevant to custody

- **Clapper v Harvey**, 716 A2d 1271 (Pa Super 1998)
  - Systematic alienation supported custody modification

### Colorado

- **In re Marriage of Plummer**, 735 P2d 165 (Colo App 1987)
  - Parent who frustrates other's parenting time acts against child's best interests

### Washington

- **In re Marriage of Woffinden**, 99 Wash App 636 (2000)
  - PA behavior can support modification even absent other changed circumstances

### New Jersey

- **Kinsella v Kinsella**, 696 A2d 556 (NJ 1997)
  - NJ Supreme Court recognized alienation as factor in custody determinations

### Arizona

- **Little v Little**, 193 Ariz 518 (1999)
  - Custodial interference and alienation warrant custody change

## V. Remedies Courts Have Ordered for Parental Alienation

- **Change of primary custody** to targeted parent
- **Reunification therapy** ordered for child and targeted parent
- **Suspension of parenting time** for alienating parent
- **Therapeutic reunification programs** (e.g., Family Bridges, Bridge the Gap)
- **Contempt findings** and jail/fines for intentional interference with court orders
- **Attorney fees and costs** awarded to targeted parent
- **Makeup parenting time** to compensate for lost time
- **GAL/CASA appointment** to represent child's best interests
- **Psychological evaluation** of alienating parent
- **Parenting coordinator** to supervise co-parenting communications

---

## VI. Expert Witness Considerations

- Experts on PA must qualify under **MRE 702 / Daubert**
- Courts distinguish between:
  - Clinical diagnosis of PAS (controversial, not in DSM-5)
  - Behavioral observations of alienating conduct (widely accepted)
- Key expert witnesses:
  - Forensic psychologist/custody evaluator
  - Child psychologist with child interview data
  - Guardian ad Litem (GAL)
  - Friend of the Court investigator
