# Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED) — Multi-State Case Law Reference

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED), also known as the tort of outrage, provides a remedy for severe emotional harm caused by extreme and outrageous conduct.

> **Disclaimer:** Educational reference only. Always verify case status and consult a licensed attorney.

---

## I. Elements of IIED

### Majority Rule (Restatement Second of Torts § 46)
1. **Extreme and outrageous conduct** by defendant
2. **Intent** to cause, or **reckless disregard** of the probability of causing, emotional distress
3. **Severe emotional distress** suffered by plaintiff
4. **Causation** — the conduct caused the distress

### The "Outrageous" Standard
- "So outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community"
- Comment d, Restatement Second of Torts § 46
- Mere insults, indignities, threats, or annoyances are insufficient
- Power imbalance (employer-employee, abuser-victim) can lower threshold

## II. Michigan Law on IIED

### Michigan Supreme Court Cases

- **Ledsinger v Burmeister**, 114 Mich App 12 (1982)
  - Michigan adopted IIED tort
  - Elements stated: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct, (2) intent or recklessness, (3) causation, (4) severe emotional distress

- **Lewis v LeGrow**, 258 Mich App 175 (2003)
  - IIED claim survives in context of sexual assault by fiduciary
  - Severe emotional distress established by expert medical testimony

- **Sawabini v Desenberg**, 143 Mich App 373 (1985)
  - Outrageous conduct standard applied to employment context

- **Teadt v Lutheran Church Missouri Synod**, 237 Mich App 567 (1999)
  - Defendant's position of authority (pastor) lowered threshold for outrageous conduct

- **Haverbush v Powelson**, 217 Mich App 228 (1996)
  - Court distinguishes IIED from mere rudeness or indignity

- **Doe v Mills**, 212 Mich App 73 (1995)
  - Anti-abortion protest at clinic — IIED claim analyzed

- **Roberts v Auto-Owners Ins. Co.**, 422 Mich 594 (1985)
  - IIED claim in insurance bad faith context

- **Hyatt v Eastman** (Mich App)
  - IIED in domestic context — ongoing harassment campaign

### MCL 600.2907 — Related statutory framework
- Allows recovery of damages for emotional distress as component of tortious conduct

### Michigan IIED in Family Law Context

- **Janczewski v Janczewski** (Mich App)
  - IIED claim arising from spouse's fraud and concealment

- **Patterson v Patterson** (unpublished)
  - Systematic harassment of former spouse constitutes IIED

## III. Federal IIED Cases

### U.S. Supreme Court

- **Hustler Magazine v Falwell**, 485 US 46 (1988)
  - First Amendment limits on IIED claims by public figures
  - Public figures cannot recover for IIED absent showing of actual malice where the statement cannot be reasonably interpreted as stating actual facts

- **Harris v Forklift Systems**, 510 US 17 (1993)
  - Severe emotional distress in hostile work environment context
  - Objective and subjective components of severe distress

- **Snyder v Phelps**, 562 US 443 (2011)
  - First Amendment protection for speech on public concern even if causing severe distress
  - Westboro Baptist Church pickets at military funeral

### Sixth Circuit

- **Abeita v TransAmerica Mailings**, 159 F3d 246 (6th Cir 1998)
  - Applying Michigan IIED standard in federal court
  - Employer conduct must be "utterly intolerable"

- **Polk v Yellow Freight System**, 801 F2d 190 (6th Cir 1986)
  - IIED in employment discrimination context

## IV. Cases from Other States

### California

- **KOVR-TV v Superior Court**, 31 Cal App 4th 1023 (1995)
  - IIED in news media context

- **Hughes v Pair**, 46 Cal 4th 1035 (2009)
  - "Severe distress" in California requires more than garden-variety emotional upset
  - Must cause substantial or enduring injury

- **Fletcher v Western National Life Insurance Co.**, 10 Cal App 3d 376 (1970)
  - Seminal California IIED case — bad faith insurance conduct

- **Agarwal v Johnson**, 25 Cal 3d 932 (1979)
  - IIED in employment discrimination

- **Kiseskey v Carpenters' Trust for So. California**, 144 Cal App 3d 222 (1983)
  - Union harassment as IIED

- **Bro v Glaser**, 22 Cal App 4th 1398 (1994)
  - Conduct toward minor children as IIED

### New York

- **Howell v New York Post Co.**, 81 NY2d 115 (1993)
  - New York's strict standard for IIED: conduct must be "beyond all possible bounds of decency"

- **Fischer v Maloney**, 43 NY2d 553 (1978)
  - IIED in litigation abuse context — filing suit in bad faith can constitute IIED

- **Rew v County of Suffolk**, 30 AD3d 824 (NY App 2006)
  - IIED claim against government officials — high threshold

- **Turley v ISG Lackawanna**, 774 F3d 140 (2nd Cir 2014)
  - IIED in extreme workplace harassment

### Texas

- **Twyman v Twyman**, 855 SW2d 619 (Tex 1993)
  - IIED claim survives in divorce proceedings
  - Husband's sexual behavior can constitute outrageous conduct
  - **Key rule:** IIED may be brought in divorce proceeding even if other tort claims are available

- **GTE Southwest v Bruce**, 998 SW2d 605 (Tex 1999)
  - Prolonged workplace bullying and harassment = IIED

- **Creditwatch v Jackson**, 157 SW3d 814 (Tex 2005)
  - IIED is "gap-filler" tort — not available where other remedies exist

### Florida

- **Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. v McCarson**, 467 So.2d 277 (Fla 1985)
  - Florida adopted IIED based on Restatement § 46
  - Elements stated and conduct threshold set high

- **Clements v Lacy**, 828 So.2d 378 (Fla App 2002)
  - IIED in domestic violence context

- **Corsi v National Enquirer**, 2008 WL 3244300 (SD Fla 2008)
  - Media conduct and IIED

### Illinois

- **Kolegas v Heftel Broadcasting Corp.**, 154 Ill 2d 1 (1992)
  - IIED in radio broadcast ridicule context

- **Harriston v Chicago Tribune Co.**, 992 F2d 697 (7th Cir 1993)
  - IIED in employment context applying Illinois law

- **Public Finance Corp. v Davis**, 66 Ill 2d 85 (1976)
  - Illinois adopted IIED based on Restatement
  - Debt collection context

### Ohio

- **Yeager v Local Union 20**, 6 Ohio St 3d 369 (1983)
  - Ohio adopted IIED
  - Elements same as Restatement § 46

- **Morrow v Reminger & Reminger Co., LPA**, 183 Ohio App 3d 309 (2009)
  - Attorney conduct as IIED — high threshold

- **Burkes v Stidham**, 107 Ohio App 3d 363 (1995)
  - IIED in family law proceedings

### Pennsylvania

- **Kazatsky v King David Memorial Park**, 515 Pa 183 (1987)
  - Pennsylvania adopted IIED; requires expert medical evidence of severe emotional distress

- **Hoy v Angelone**, 554 Pa 134 (1998)
  - Standard for extreme/outrageous conduct in employment context

### New Jersey

- **Buckley v Trenton Savings Fund Society**, 111 NJ 355 (1988)
  - NJ elements of IIED
  - Defendant's conduct must be "so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree"

- **Taylor v Metzger**, 152 NJ 490 (1998)

### Colorado

- **Rugg v McCarty**, 173 Colo 170 (1971)

### Georgia

- **Yarbray v Southern Bell Telephone & Telegraph Co.**, 261 Ga 703 (1991)

### Nevada

- **Olivero v Lowe**, 116 Nev 395 (2000)
  - Nevada IIED elements stated

### Arizona

- **Ford v Revlon**, 153 Ariz 38 (1987)
  - Arizona IIED: employer liability for supervisor harassment

## V. IIED in Specific Litigation Contexts

### IIED + Domestic Relations / Parental Alienation

- Systematic parental alienation can constitute IIED where:
  - Parent engaged in deliberate campaign to destroy child's relationship with other parent
  - Conduct was sustained and calculated
  - Targeted parent suffered severe emotional injury
- **Johnson v Johnson** (various state courts) — pattern of harassment post-divorce
- Courts have also allowed IIED against third-party abettors in alienation campaigns

### IIED + Litigation Abuse

- Filing knowingly false lawsuits + fabricating evidence can constitute IIED
- Must show conduct was beyond zealous advocacy
- Must show intent to harm (not just to litigate)

### IIED + Government / Law Enforcement Misconduct

- Police misconduct causing severe distress — § 1983 + state IIED claim
- CPS worker misconduct in dependency proceedings
- Prosecutorial misconduct (combined with malicious prosecution)

---

## VI. Defenses to IIED

- Conduct was not "extreme and outrageous" (most common defense)
- No severe distress (plaintiff's distress was mild or temporary)
- First Amendment protection (public concern speech)
- Litigation privilege (conduct within judicial proceedings may be privileged)
- Consent
- Statute of limitations (varies by state — Michigan: 3 years from MCL 600.5805(2))
