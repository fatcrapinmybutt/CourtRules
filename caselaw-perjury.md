# Perjury — Multi-State Case Law Reference

Perjury is the willful making of a false statement, under oath, on a material matter in a judicial or official proceeding. It is both a crime and grounds for civil remedies including contempt, new trial, and relief from judgment.

> **Disclaimer:** Educational reference only. Always verify case status and consult a licensed attorney.

---

## I. Perjury as a Crime

### Federal Perjury Statute (18 U.S.C. § 1621)

**Elements:**
1. The defendant took an oath before a competent tribunal, officer, or person
2. The defendant made a false statement
3. The statement was material to the proceedings
4. The defendant knew the statement to be false at the time it was made (willfulness)

**Penalty:** Up to 5 years imprisonment

### Federal Subornation of Perjury (18 U.S.C. § 1622)

- Procuring another to commit perjury
- Same penalty as perjury: up to 5 years imprisonment

### Federal False Declarations Before Grand Jury or Court (18 U.S.C. § 1623)

- False material declaration under oath in federal proceedings
- Two-witness rule does NOT apply (unlike § 1621)
- Affirmative defense: timely recantation before false statement substantially affected proceeding

### Obstruction of Justice — Related Offenses

- **18 U.S.C. § 1503** — Obstruction of justice (includes perjury in federal proceedings)
- **18 U.S.C. § 1512** — Tampering with a witness, victim, or informant
- **18 U.S.C. § 1001** — False statements to federal officials (broader scope than perjury)

## II. Michigan Perjury Statutes

### MCL 750.422 — Perjury

> "Any person who, being lawfully required to depose the truth in any proceeding in a court of justice, shall commit perjury shall be guilty of a felony punishable by imprisonment in the state prison for not more than 15 years."

**Elements under Michigan law:**
1. Lawfully required to depose the truth in a court proceeding
2. Made a false statement
3. The statement was material
4. Made corruptly and willfully

### MCL 750.423 — Subornation of Perjury

- Procuring another to commit perjury
- Same penalty as perjury: up to 15 years

### MCL 750.425 — False Swearing

- False oath not in judicial proceeding but before notary or officer
- Misdemeanor

### MCL 750.158 — Conspiracy to Commit Perjury

- Conspiracy to procure false testimony
- Felony

### MCL 600.1711 — Contempt Based on False Testimony

- Court may hold witness in contempt for perjury
- Civil or criminal contempt sanctions available

## III. Federal Cases on Perjury

### U.S. Supreme Court

- **Bronston v United States**, 409 US 352 (1973)
  - **Landmark ruling:** Literally true but misleading answer is NOT perjury under federal law
  - Perjury requires a false statement, not merely a misleading one
  - "A witness may not be convicted of perjury for an answer, under oath, that is literally true but not responsive to the question asked"
  - Remedy for misleading non-answers is vigorous cross-examination, not perjury prosecution

- **United States v Dunnigan**, 507 US 87 (1993)
  - Defendant who commits perjury at trial may receive sentencing enhancement
  - Court must make specific findings that defendant willfully gave false testimony on material matter

- **United States v Norris**, 300 US 564 (1937)
  - Materiality standard in federal perjury
  - False statement must have a "natural tendency to influence, or [be] capable of influencing," the tribunal

- **In re Michael**, 326 US 224 (1945)
  - Perjury standard — willfulness requirement

- **United States v Berger**, 295 US 78 (1935)
  - Prosecutor's duty of candor — perjured testimony known to government cannot be used

- **Napue v Illinois**, 360 US 264 (1959)
  - Conviction obtained through knowing use of perjured testimony violates due process
  - Even if perjury goes only to credibility of witness

- **Mooney v Holohan**, 294 US 103 (1935)
  - Due process prohibits conviction based on deliberate deception of court and jury through false testimony

### Sixth Circuit

- **United States v Swinehart**, 614 F2d 853 (6th Cir 1980)
  - Two-witness rule in federal perjury under § 1621
  - Required: either (1) two witnesses or (2) one witness plus corroborating circumstances

- **United States v McKenna**, 327 F3d 830 (9th Cir 2003)
  - Materiality analysis in perjury cases

- **United States v Weimert**, 819 F3d 351 (7th Cir 2016)
  - Perjury in civil context analyzed

## IV. Michigan Cases on Perjury

### Michigan Supreme Court

- **People v Lively**, 470 Mich 248 (2004)
  - Perjury elements restated under MCL 750.422
  - Materiality: "reasonably likely to affect the outcome of the proceeding"

- **People v Kowalski**, 489 Mich 488 (2011)
  - False testimony in criminal proceedings
  - Court's ability to address perjury through contempt and criminal referral

- **People v Burden**, 395 Mich 462 (1975)
  - Corroboration requirements in Michigan perjury prosecutions

- **People v Moriarity**, 283 Mich App 684 (2009)
  - Materiality — when false statement affects judicial determination

- **People v Haynes**, 65 Mich App 732 (1975)
  - Perjury — willfulness requirement

### Michigan Court of Appeals

- **People v Hawthorne**, 474 Mich 174 (2006)
  - Perjury in context of false testimony at trial

- **People v Ecker**, 2015 WL 4717459 (Mich App 2015) *(unpub)*
  - Perjury prosecution for false statements at evidentiary hearing

### Michigan Perjury in Civil and Family Court Context

- **MCL 552.12** — False statements in domestic relations proceedings can support criminal referral
- **Friend of the Court investigations** — false statements constitute perjury under oath
- **Referee hearings** — witnesses are under oath; perjury statutes apply
- **Affidavits** — sworn affidavits filed with the court are subject to perjury statutes when false
- **FRCP 56(h) / MCR 2.116** — If affidavit in summary judgment proceedings is perjured, court may impose sanctions including contempt, costs, attorney fees

## VI. Civil Remedies for Perjury in Judicial Proceedings

### Relief from Judgment Based on Perjury

- **FRCP 60(b)(3):** Fraud, misrepresentation, or misconduct by opposing party — must file within **1 year**
- **FRCP 60(d)(3):** Fraud on the court — **no time limit**
- **MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c):** Michigan equivalent — within 1 year
- **MCR 2.612(C)(3):** Void judgment — no time limit

### Standards for Granting Relief Based on Perjury

- Perjury must have been material to outcome
- Perjury must have affected the judgment
- New evidence of perjury discovered after judgment
- Requires showing by **clear and convincing evidence**

### Contempt for Perjury

- **MCL 600.1711** — Direct criminal contempt of court
- Court may find witness in contempt after perjury is established
- Punishment: fine, imprisonment, or both

### Sanctions Under Court Rules

- **MCR 2.114** / **FRCP 11** — Sanctions for false certifications
- **MCR 2.313** / **FRCP 37** — Discovery sanctions for false sworn responses
- **Inherent power** of court to sanction perjury through dismissal, default, adverse inference, fee award

## VII. Two-Witness Rule in Perjury Prosecutions

### Federal Law

- Under **18 U.S.C. § 1621**, perjury cannot be proved by the testimony of a single witness alone
- Requires: **two witnesses** corroborating falsity, OR **one witness + independent corroborating evidence**
- Does NOT apply under **18 U.S.C. § 1623** (false declarations in federal proceedings)

### Michigan Law

- Michigan does not follow a strict two-witness rule for all perjury prosecutions
- Corroboration still generally required — perjury must be proved beyond a reasonable doubt
- *People v Burden*, 395 Mich 462 (1975)

---

## VIII. Collateral Estoppel and Perjury

- Prior finding of perjury in one proceeding may have collateral estoppel effect in subsequent related proceeding
- Professional discipline proceedings rely on criminal perjury conviction
- Civil courts may draw adverse inference from perjury in related criminal proceeding
