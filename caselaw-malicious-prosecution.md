# Malicious Prosecution — Multi-State Case Law Reference

Malicious prosecution is a tort (and in some contexts a constitutional claim) arising when a party initiates or continues legal proceedings without probable cause, with malice, and the proceedings terminate in the defendant's favor.

> **Disclaimer:** Educational reference only. Always verify case status and consult a licensed attorney.

---

## I. Elements of Malicious Prosecution

### Common Law Elements (majority rule)
1. **Prior proceeding** — criminal or civil action was initiated or continued by defendant
2. **Termination in plaintiff's favor** — prior proceeding ended favorably to plaintiff
3. **Absence of probable cause** — defendant lacked reasonable basis for the prior proceeding
4. **Malice** — defendant acted with improper motive (ill will, spite, or purpose other than that for which the proceeding is designed)
5. **Damages** — plaintiff suffered actual injury

### Civil vs. Criminal Malicious Prosecution
- **Criminal malicious prosecution:** Wrongful initiation of criminal charges
- **Civil malicious prosecution** (also called "wrongful use of civil proceedings"): Wrongful filing of civil lawsuit
- **Abuse of process:** Related but distinct — misuse of legal process for improper purpose (does not require termination in plaintiff's favor)

## II. Michigan Law on Malicious Prosecution

### Michigan Supreme Court Cases

- **Young v Thendara**, 14 Mich 340 (1866)
  - Early Michigan statement of malicious prosecution elements

- **Friedman v Dozorc**, 412 Mich 1 (1981)
  - **Leading Michigan case**
  - "To establish a claim for malicious prosecution in a civil context, a plaintiff must show that (1) defendant initiated a prior civil proceeding, (2) the proceeding terminated in plaintiff's favor, (3) defendant lacked probable cause to initiate the proceeding, and (4) defendant acted with malice"
  - Recognized cause of action for wrongful use of civil process

- **Ponca Indian Tribe v Mich. Dep't of Treasury** (Mich 2003)
  - State actor immunity considerations in malicious prosecution

- **Stone v Williamson**, 131 Mich App 226 (1983)
  - Elements of civil malicious prosecution restated

### Michigan Court of Appeals Cases

- **Slade v Providence-St. Joseph Health System**, unpub
  - Malicious prosecution in medical malpractice context

- **Massey v Hathaway**, 2010 WL 3123539 (Mich App 2010)
  - Probable cause analysis in civil malicious prosecution

- **Prysak v RL Polk Co**, 193 Mich App 1 (1992)
  - Employer-initiated proceedings; abuse of process distinguished

- **MacDonald v MacNaughton**, 2014 WL 3893820 (Mich App 2014) *(unpublished)*
  - False police report forming basis of malicious prosecution

### MCL 600.2907 — Michigan Statute: Malicious Prosecution
- Provides cause of action for malicious prosecution of civil proceedings
- Damages include: humiliation, injury to reputation, attorney fees, emotional distress

## III. Federal Section 1983 Malicious Prosecution Claims

### U.S. Supreme Court

- **Heck v Humphrey**, 512 US 477 (1994)
  - **Critical rule:** Section 1983 malicious prosecution claim cannot be brought if it would imply invalidity of existing conviction
  - Must obtain favorable termination first (reversal, expungement, pardon, etc.)

- **Manuel v City of Joliet**, 580 US 357 (2017)
  - Fourth Amendment basis for pretrial detention without probable cause
  - Extends protection through legal proceedings, not just arrest

- **Thompson v Clark**, 596 US 36 (2022)
  - **Important recent ruling:** Favorable termination element satisfied when prosecution ends without conviction on the merits; dismissal in interest of justice may suffice

- **McDonough v Smith**, 139 S Ct 2149 (2019)
  - Section 1983 fabricated evidence claim accrues when criminal proceeding terminates favorably

- **Albright v Oliver**, 510 US 266 (1994)
  - Malicious prosecution as constitutional claim; plurality opinion fragmented on constitutional basis

### Sixth Circuit (Michigan federal cases)

- **Fox v DeSoto**, 489 F3d 227 (6th Cir 2007)
  - Elements of § 1983 Fourth Amendment malicious prosecution
  - (1) criminal prosecution instituted against plaintiff, (2) without probable cause, (3) by defendant acting under color of state law, (4) terminated in plaintiff's favor, (5) resulting in deprivation of liberty

- **Sykes v Anderson**, 625 F3d 294 (6th Cir 2010)
  - Sixth Circuit's standard for § 1983 malicious prosecution restated
  - Distinguishes between arrest and post-arraignment detention without probable cause

- **King v Harwood**, 852 F3d 568 (6th Cir 2017)
  - Favorable termination element analyzed

- **Mills v Barnard**, 869 F3d 473 (6th Cir 2017)
  - Evidence fabrication as malicious prosecution element

- **Johnson v Moseley**, 790 F3d 649 (6th Cir 2015)
  - Malicious prosecution claim by plaintiff acquitted of criminal charges

## IV. Cases from Other States

### California

- **Sheldon Appel Co. v Albert & Oliker**, 47 Cal 3d 863 (1989)
  - Elements of malicious prosecution in California
  - Probable cause defined: "whether any reasonable attorney would have thought the claim tenable"
  - Objective standard for probable cause

- **Zamos v Stroud**, 32 Cal 4th 958 (2004)
  - Attorney liability for continuing to prosecute action after discovering it lacks merit

- **Soukup v Law Offices of Herbert Hafif**, 39 Cal 4th 260 (2006)
  - Anti-SLAPP statute intersection with malicious prosecution

- **Jay v Mahaffey**, 218 Cal App 4th 1522 (2013)
  - Requirements for malicious prosecution claim in California summarized

### New York

- **Broughton v State of New York**, 37 NY2d 451 (1975)
  - Elements of malicious prosecution in New York restated
  - Favorable termination required: must be determination on merits

- **Savino v City of New York**, 331 F3d 63 (2nd Cir 2003)
  - Probable cause is complete defense to malicious prosecution

- **Nardelli v Stamberg**, 44 NY2d 500 (1978)
  - Malice element — "motivated by spite, ill will or other bad faith"

- **Smith-Hunter v Harvey**, 95 NY2d 191 (2000)
  - Favorable termination — dismissal on merits vs. procedural grounds

### Texas

- **Kroger Texas Ltd. Partnership v Suberu**, 216 SW3d 788 (Tex 2006)
  - Texas elements of malicious prosecution
  - Probable cause is objective standard
  - Malice can be inferred from absence of probable cause

- **Richey v Brookshire Grocery Co.**, 952 SW2d 515 (Tex 1997)
  - Employer who directs prosecution can be liable for malicious prosecution

### Florida

- **Alamo Rent-A-Car v Mancusi**, 632 So.2d 1352 (Fla 1994)
  - Florida elements of malicious prosecution

- **Burns v GCC Beverages**, 502 So.2d 1217 (Fla 1987)
  - Probable cause defined in Florida malicious prosecution context

- **Nadeau v State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins.**, 2016 WL 5376280 (Fla App 2016)
  - Insurer liability for malicious prosecution claims

### Illinois

- **Swick v Liautaud**, 169 Ill 2d 504 (1996)
  - Favorable termination element in Illinois

- **Beaman v Freesands Evans & Lewis**, 2012 IL 110867
  - Illinois Supreme Court on attorney liability for malicious prosecution

### Pennsylvania

- **Kelley v General Teamsters Local 249**, 544 Pa 360 (1996)
  - Pennsylvania elements of malicious prosecution restated

- **McNeil v Owens-Corning Fiberglas Corp.**, 545 Pa 209 (1996)
  - Burden of proof on each element

### Ohio

- **Trussell v General Motors Corp.**, 53 Ohio St 3d 142 (1990)
  - Ohio elements: (1) malicious institution of prior proceedings, (2) lack of probable cause, (3) termination in plaintiff's favor, (4) seizure of plaintiff's person or property

- **Crawford v Euclid Nat'l Bank**, 19 Ohio St 3d 203 (1985)
  - Probable cause standard in Ohio

### Georgia

- **Yost v Torok**, 256 Ga 92 (1986)
  - Georgia's "abusive litigation" statute in context of malicious prosecution

- **Vogtle v Coleman**, 259 Ga 115 (1989)
  - Elements of malicious prosecution in Georgia

## V. Special Topics

### Malicious Prosecution in Family Court Context

- **Filing false CPS reports:** Can support malicious prosecution if knowingly false
  - *See:* Brady v Maryland (suppression of exculpatory evidence)
  - False abuse allegations in custody proceedings → malicious prosecution + fraud on court
- **Frivolous motions for PPOs/restraining orders:** May support abuse of process or malicious prosecution
- **False paternity actions:** Malicious prosecution where paternity claim knowingly frivolous

### Absolute vs. Qualified Immunity

- **Prosecutors** have **absolute immunity** for acts within prosecutorial function (*Imbler v Pachtman*, 424 US 409 (1976))
  - Exception: investigative acts (not protected by absolute immunity)
- **Police officers** have **qualified immunity** unless violated clearly established rights
  - *Pearson v Callahan*, 555 US 223 (2009)
- **Private individuals** — no immunity

### Wrongful Use of Civil Process (Restatement Second of Torts § 674)

- Primary purpose was not to succeed on the merits
- Initiated without reasonable belief in the claim
- Prior proceeding terminated in defendant's favor

---

## VI. Damages in Malicious Prosecution

- **Special damages:** Attorney fees in defending prior proceeding, lost wages
- **General damages:** Emotional distress, humiliation, reputational harm
- **Punitive damages:** Available in most states where malice is proven
- **Attorney fees:** Available under 42 U.S.C. § 1988 in § 1983 claims
