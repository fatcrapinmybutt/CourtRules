# Fraud on the Court — Multi-State Case Law Reference

Fraud on the court (also called "fraud upon the court") is a species of fraud that occurs when a party or officer of the court commits fraud that undermines the integrity of the judicial process itself. It is among the most serious forms of misconduct recognized by courts.

> **Disclaimer:** Educational reference only. Always verify case status and consult a licensed attorney.

---

## I. Defining Fraud on the Court

### Classic Definition

> "Fraud upon the court occurs where it can be demonstrated, clearly and convincingly, that a party has sentiently set in motion some unconscionable scheme calculated to interfere with the judicial system's ability impartially to adjudicate a matter by improperly influencing the trier of fact or unfairly hampering the presentation of the opposing party's claim or defense."
>
> — *Aoude v Mobil Oil Corp.*, 892 F2d 1115, 1118 (1st Cir 1989)

### Distinguished From Ordinary Fraud

| Ordinary Fraud | Fraud on the Court |
|---|---|
| Between parties | Directed at the court itself |
| Governed by FRCP 60(b)(3) — 1-year limit | No time limit (inherent power) |
| Requires showing by clear and convincing evidence | Also clear and convincing |
| Remedied by vacating judgment | May result in dismissal, sanctions, contempt |

---

## II. Key Legal Standards

### Federal Standard (FRCP 60(b))
- **FRCP 60(b)(3):** Relief from judgment for "fraud (whether previously called intrinsic or extrinsic), misrepresentation, or misconduct by an opposing party"
  - **1-year limitation** from entry of judgment
- **FRCP 60(d)(3):** Court may set aside judgment for "fraud on the court" — **no time limit**
  - Reserved for the most egregious conduct
  - Requires affirmative conduct undermining the court's integrity

### Michigan Standard (MCR 2.612)
- **MCR 2.612(C)(1)(c):** Relief from judgment for fraud, misrepresentation, or misconduct — within **1 year**
- **MCR 2.612(C)(3):** Power to set aside void judgment — **no time limit**
- **Inherent power** of Michigan courts to address fraud on the court regardless of timing

## III. Federal Cases on Fraud on the Court

### U.S. Supreme Court

- **Hazel-Atlas Glass Co. v Hartford-Empire Co.**, 322 US 238 (1944)
  - **Landmark case:** U.S. Supreme Court vacated judgment 11 years after entry
  - Fraud consisted of fabricating article by ghostwriting to create false evidence supporting patent claim
  - "The public welfare demands that the agencies of public justice be not so impotent that they must always be mute and helpless victims of deception and fraud"
  - **No time limit on power to address fraud on court**

- **Universal Oil Products Co. v Root Refining Co.**, 328 US 575 (1946)
  - Extended Hazel-Atlas; courts have inherent equitable power to correct fraud

- **Chambers v NASCO**, 501 US 32 (1991)
  - Courts' inherent power to sanction bad faith conduct including fraud
  - Source of power: inherent authority, not just rules
  - Attorney fees awarded as sanction for fraud on court

- **Link v Wabash Railroad Co.**, 370 US 626 (1962)
  - Inherent powers of courts include dismissal for party misconduct

### Circuit Courts

- **Aoude v Mobil Oil Corp.**, 892 F2d 1115 (1st Cir 1989)
  - Definitive formulation of fraud on court standard
  - Must be "unconscionable scheme" interfering with fair adjudication

- **Kupferman v Consolidated Research & Manufacturing Corp.**, 459 F2d 1072 (2nd Cir 1972)
  - Fraud must "defile" the court itself, not merely deceive opposing party

- **Demjanjuk v Petrovsky**, 10 F3d 338 (6th Cir 1993)
  - **Sixth Circuit:** Fraud on court must involve scheme to interfere with "the ability of the court to impartially adjudicate"
  - Government's failure to disclose exculpatory evidence can constitute fraud on court

- **Herring v United States**, 424 F3d 384 (3rd Cir 2005)
  - Clear and convincing evidence standard required
  - Fraud must be directed at court, not just parties

- **Rozier v Ford Motor Co.**, 573 F2d 1332 (5th Cir 1978)
  - Fraud must involve more than nondisclosure (absent duty to disclose)
  - Must involve active misrepresentation

- **Cleveland Demolition Co. v Azcon Scrap Corp.**, 827 F2d 984 (4th Cir 1987)
  - Perjury alone insufficient unless pervasive and central to outcome

- **Robinson v Audi Aktiengesellschaft**, 56 F3d 1259 (10th Cir 1995)
  - Must show opposing party deliberately engaged in fraud scheme targeting court's impartial adjudicative function

- **Apotex Corp. v Merck & Co.**, 507 F3d 1357 (Fed Cir 2007)
  - Fraud in patent litigation; fabricated prior art = fraud on court

### Sixth Circuit Cases

- **Carter v Anderson**, 585 F3d 1007 (6th Cir 2009)
  - Standard for fraud on court in Sixth Circuit restated

- **Johnson v Bell**, 605 F3d 333 (6th Cir 2010)
  - Government misconduct in criminal proceedings as fraud on court

- **Stone v City of Chicago**, 738 F2d 896 (7th Cir 1984)
  - Perjury by party + attorney knowledge = fraud on court

## IV. Michigan Cases on Fraud on the Court

### Michigan Supreme Court

- **Sprague v Buhagiar**, 213 Mich App 310 (1995)
  - Michigan recognizes fraud on court based on inherent judicial power
  - "A court's equitable power to set aside a judgment procured by fraud is not limited by MCR 2.612 when the fraud is fraud upon the court"

- **Triplett v St. Amour**, 444 Mich 170 (1993)
  - Court discussed equitable power to set aside judgments obtained through fraud

- **Tora v Mich. Workers' Compensation Appellate Commission** (Mich App)
  - Fraud on administrative tribunal extends same principles

### Michigan Court of Appeals

- **Cain v Dep't of Corrections**, 451 Mich 470 (1996)
  - Government disclosure obligations and fraud on court

- **Mettler Walloon v Melrose Township**, 281 Mich App 184 (2008)
  - Attorney misconduct amounting to fraud on court

- **Rose v Cochran**, unpublished (Mich App)
  - Fraudulent misrepresentation in custody proceedings = fraud on court
  - False evidence submitted to family court

- **In re Estate of Buckley** (Mich App, various years)
  - Fraud on probate court through false filings

### Michigan: Fraud on Court in Family/Custody Cases

- False statements in domestic relations pleadings
- Fabricated evidence of abuse (photos, medical records)
- Concealed assets in divorce proceedings
- Perjured testimony before referee or judge
- All may constitute fraud on court with no time limitation for seeking relief under MCR 2.612(C)(3) or inherent power doctrine

## V. Cases from Other States

### California

- **In re Marriage of Rosevear**, 65 Cal App 4th 673 (1998)
  - Fraud on court in dissolution proceedings — perjury + concealment of assets
  - Court has inherent power to set aside judgment

- **Weitz v Yankosky**, 63 Cal 2d 849 (1965)
  - Seminal California case on fraud upon the court

- **Rubenstein v Rubenstein**, 81 Cal App 4th 1131 (2000)
  - Concealment of assets in divorce = fraud on court

- **In re Marriage of Kieturakis**, 138 Cal App 4th 56 (2006)
  - Standard for fraud on court in family law proceedings

### New York

- **Matter of Estate of Sasson**, 12 Misc3d 1131(A) (2006)
  - Fraud on court — probate proceedings

- **Kessler v Kessler**, 2006 WL 723600 (NY App 2006)
  - Concealment in matrimonial proceedings = fraud on court

### Florida

- **Cox v Burke**, 706 So.2d 43 (Fla App 1998)
  - Fraud on court: fabrication of evidence + suborning perjury
  - Court has inherent power to set aside judgment obtained by fraud

- **Martino v Wal-Mart Stores**, 908 So.2d 342 (Fla 2005)
  - Fraud on court standard in Florida: must involve "unconscionable scheme"

### Texas

- **Golden v Golden**, 145 SW3d 212 (Tex App 2004)
  - Texas courts have inherent power to vacate void judgments obtained by fraud

- **Tice v City of Pasadena**, 767 SW2d 700 (Tex 1989)
  - Fraud must be extrinsic to be actionable post-judgment

### Illinois

- **Smith v Airoom**, 114 Ill 2d 209 (1986)
  - Fraud on court standard in Illinois
  - Must be extrinsic fraud (outside the fact-finding process) for post-judgment relief

### Ohio

- **Strack v Pelton**, 70 Ohio St 3d 172 (1994)
  - Ohio: courts have inherent authority to vacate judgments procured by fraud

### Pennsylvania

- **Lazendorf v United States**, 2009 WL 1543893 (MD Pa 2009)
  - Fraud on court in federal court sitting in Pennsylvania

## VI. Fraud on Court by Officers of the Court

Courts give special attention when officers of the court (attorneys) are involved:

- **Attorney suborning perjury**
  - *In re Disciplinary Proceeding Against Haskell*, 962 P2d 813 (Wash 1998)

- **Attorney making false representations to court**
  - MRPC 3.3 (Michigan) — Candor toward tribunal
  - "A lawyer shall not knowingly make a false statement of fact or law to a tribunal"
  - "A lawyer shall not offer evidence the lawyer knows to be false"

- **Attorney knowingly using perjured testimony**
  - *Williamson v Moore*, 221 F3d 1177 (11th Cir 2000)

- **Government attorney withholding Brady material**
  - *United States v Agurs*, 427 US 97 (1976)
  - *Brady v Maryland*, 373 US 83 (1963)
  - *Giglio v United States*, 405 US 150 (1972)

## VII. Remedies for Fraud on the Court

- **Vacating or setting aside the judgment** — MCR 2.612(C)(3); FRCP 60(d)(3)
- **Dismissal of the offending party's claims** — as sanction
- **Default judgment** — against the fraudulent party
- **Civil contempt** — sanctions, fines, imprisonment until compliance
- **Criminal referral** — for perjury, obstruction
- **Attorney discipline** — State Bar grievance; suspension or disbarment
- **Fee shifting** — attorney fees and costs under court's inherent power or § 1988
- **New trial** — if fraud affected jury verdict
- **Collateral attack** — independent action in equity to set aside fraudulent judgment
