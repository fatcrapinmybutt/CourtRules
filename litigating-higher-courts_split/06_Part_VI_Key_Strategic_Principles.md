## Part VI — Key Strategic Principles

### Preservation Is Everything

- Every appellate argument must have been raised and ruled on below
- Failure to preserve = waiver (unless plain error)
- Always make a clear record: "I object on the grounds that..."
- File written objections to referee recommendations (MCR 3.209(E))
- File objections to magistrate's R&R (LCivR 72.2)

### Identify the Right Standard of Review

- De novo review (no deference to trial court) — best for appellant
- Abuse of discretion review — very deferential; hard to win
- Frame every issue to get de novo review if possible
- "The court misapplied the law" → de novo
- "The court made wrong factual finding" → clear error (harder to win)

### Building an Appellate Record

- **Trial counsel must think like an appellate lawyer**
- Create a clear record of every ruling you want reviewed
- File written motions even if you expect to lose
- Obtain written orders on all rulings
- Ensure transcript is accurate; order corrections promptly

### Writing Persuasively

- Courts decide cases based on facts more often than lawyers realize — tell a compelling story
- Cite directly to the record on every factual assertion
- Lead with your strongest argument
- State the rule clearly before applying it
- Be honest about weaknesses — address them; do not hide them
- Write for a judge who knows nothing about your case
- Edit ruthlessly: shorter briefs that make the point clearly are more effective

### Using Unpublished Opinions

- Michigan COA unpublished opinions: not binding but may be cited for persuasive value (MCR 7.215(J)(1))
- Sixth Circuit unpublished: may be cited since 2007 (FRAP 32.1)
- U.S. Supreme Court: all opinions published and binding

### Handling Adverse Controlling Precedent

- Distinguish on facts (most common and effective)
- Argue ruling was limited to specific circumstances
- Argue controlling precedent has been implicitly overruled or undermined
- Ask court to reconsider — frame as invitation to revisit rather than direct attack
- Preserve issue for U.S. Supreme Court certiorari
