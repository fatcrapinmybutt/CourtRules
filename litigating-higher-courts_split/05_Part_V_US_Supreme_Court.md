## Part V — U.S. Supreme Court

### Petition for Certiorari

**When to File:**
- After Sixth Circuit denies rehearing en banc or issues final judgment
- Deadline: **90 days** from entry of judgment or denial of rehearing

**Filing Requirements (Supreme Court Rule 14):**
- 40 pages maximum for petition
- Must include:
  - Questions presented (lead with this — it is the most important page)
  - Table of contents
  - Table of authorities
  - Opinions below (COA opinion and district court opinion)
  - Jurisdiction statement
  - Constitutional/statutory provisions involved
  - Statement of the case (concise)
  - Reasons for granting certiorari
  - Conclusion (precise relief sought)
  - Appendix (opinions of lower courts)

**Filing Fee:** $300
**In Forma Pauperis:** Waiver available (Rule 39)

### Grounds for Certiorari (Supreme Court Rule 10)

> "A petition for a writ of certiorari is rarely granted when the asserted error consists of erroneous factual findings or the misapplication of a properly stated rule of law."

Strong grounds include:
- **Circuit split** — two or more circuits disagree on a federal question
- **State court and federal court conflict** — state court of last resort decided federal question contrary to Supreme Court decisions
- **Significant federal question** not yet settled by Supreme Court
- **Supreme Court precedent directly applicable** and court below departed from it

### After Certiorari is Granted

1. Joint Appendix — compiled by parties (includes key portions of record)
2. Opening brief on the merits — **50 pages**
3. Response brief — **50 pages**
4. Reply brief — **20 pages**
5. Oral argument — **30 minutes per side** (rarely extended)
6. Decision — opinion binding on all courts in U.S.

### Pro Se Petitioners

- May file under Rule 39 (in forma pauperis)
- "Paid" petitions: formal booklet format required (6⅛ × 9¼, saddle-stitched, specific cover colors)
- IFP petitions: 8½ × 11 paper format acceptable
- Clerk of Supreme Court will not accept non-compliant petitions

### Certiorari Grant Rate

- Approximately **1–2%** of petitions granted
- Court receives ~8,000 petitions per term
- Grants ~70–80 cases per term (about 1%)
