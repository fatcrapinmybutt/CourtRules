# Litigating Higher Court Cases: Requirements and Strategy

This guide covers the requirements, procedures, and strategic considerations for litigating in Michigan appellate courts, the Sixth Circuit, and the U.S. Supreme Court.

---

## Part I — Michigan Court of Appeals

### When You Can Appeal to the COA

**Appeals of Right (MCR 7.203(A)):**
- Final judgment or order of a circuit court
- Final order of a probate court
- Order granting or denying new trial in criminal cases
- Certain interlocutory orders affecting substantial rights

**Leave to Appeal (MCR 7.203(B)):**
- Interlocutory orders not yet final
- Orders from circuit court where appeal of right does not exist
- Appeals from district court (in most civil cases, appeal to circuit first, then COA)

### Step-by-Step: Civil Appeal to COA

1. **File claim of appeal** — MCR 7.204
   - Within **21 days** of entry of final order
   - File in trial court AND serve on all parties
   - Pay **$375 filing fee** (verify current amount)

2. **Order transcript** — MCR 7.204(F)
   - Must order within **7 days** of filing claim
   - Transcript of all relevant proceedings is critical

3. **Record transmitted** — Trial court clerk sends record to COA

4. **File appellant's brief** — MCR 7.210(B)
   - Due **56 days** after claim of appeal docketed (or after transcript filed)
   - Required contents: table of contents, table of authorities, statement of questions presented, statement of facts, argument, relief requested
   - **50-page maximum** (or 16,000 words)

5. **Appellee's brief** — due **35 days** after appellant's brief served

6. **Reply brief** — optional; due **21 days** after appellee's brief served

7. **Oral argument** — may be requested; court decides whether to grant

8. **Decision** — COA issues opinion (published or unpublished)

9. **Reconsideration** — motion within **21 days** of opinion

10. **Application to Supreme Court** — within **56 days** of COA decision

### How to Write a Winning Brief

- **Issues presented:** Craft questions that favor your answer when read; be specific
- **Statement of facts:** Tell the story — be accurate, chronological, cite record on every factual assertion
- **Standard of review:** Identify correct standard for each issue:
  - De novo — questions of law, constitutional questions, MCR 2.116(C)(8) and (C)(10) (summary disposition)
  - Abuse of discretion — evidentiary rulings, sanctions, case management
  - Clear error — trial court factual findings
  - Clearly erroneous — findings by court in non-jury trial
- **Argument section:**
  - Begin each argument with a clear statement of the rule of law
  - Apply the rule to your specific facts
  - Distinguish adverse cases; distinguish on facts or narrow the rule
  - Address the strongest counterarguments — courts trust advocates who acknowledge problems
- **Conclusion:** State exactly what relief you want (reverse, remand with instructions, affirm)

### Common Grounds for Reversal

- Trial court misapplied law (de novo review — easiest standard for appellant)
- Constitutional error (structural error requires automatic reversal; harmless error analysis otherwise)
- Abuse of discretion in evidentiary rulings that affected outcome
- Insufficient evidence to support verdict (viewed in light most favorable to verdict — hard standard)
- Manifest weight of evidence against verdict
- Jury instruction error — affects outcome
- Failure to grant summary disposition where no genuine issue of material fact
- Fraud on the court / perjury affecting result

### Preservation of Issues

> **Critical rule: Issues not raised below are generally waived on appeal.**

- Must raise issue in trial court; get a ruling
- Object to jury instructions timely (MCR 2.512(C))
- Object to evidence timely (MRE 103)
- File post-trial motion (JNOV / new trial) to preserve sufficiency and weight issues
- Exception: Plain error affecting substantial rights may be reviewed even if not preserved

## Part II — Michigan Supreme Court

### Overview

- Michigan Supreme Court is highest state court
- 7 justices (elected in nonpartisan elections)
- Must seek *leave to appeal* (not appeal of right in most cases)
- Grants leave only in cases of significant public interest, conflicts in COA, or substantial departures from controlling law

### Application for Leave to Appeal — MCR 7.305

**Filing Deadline:**
- **56 days** from COA opinion/order
- Delayed applications: within **6 months** with good cause

**Required Contents (MCR 7.302(C)):**
- Concise statement of facts
- Statement of questions presented
- Argument for why leave should be granted — must address MCR 7.302(D) factors:
  - Conflict with prior Supreme Court decisions
  - Conflict between COA opinions
  - Significant public interest
  - Substantial departures from law
  - Significant constitutional questions
- Copy of COA opinion
- Copy of trial court judgment
- Table of contents and authorities

**Tip:** Application for leave is a **persuasion document.** Do not just re-argue your appeal. Frame the issue as important beyond your case.

### Response to Application

- Appellee has **28 days** to file response
- May cross-apply within **21 days** of original application

### After Leave is Granted

1. Court issues order granting leave and specifying issues
2. Briefing schedule established
3. Principal briefs — same format as COA; **50 pages / 16,000 words**
4. Oral argument typically **30 minutes per side**
5. Decision — binding on all Michigan courts (stare decisis)

### Supreme Court Bypass (MCR 7.304)

- For cases of exceptional public importance
- Filed directly with Supreme Court, skipping COA
- Within **42 days** of final circuit court judgment
- Rarely granted — must show truly exceptional importance

## Part III — U.S. District Court (Western and Eastern Districts of Michigan)

### When Federal Courts Have Jurisdiction

**Federal Question Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. § 1331):**
- Claims arising under U.S. Constitution, federal statutes, or treaties
- Common bases:
  - 42 U.S.C. § 1983 (civil rights violations under color of state law)
  - Title VII (employment discrimination)
  - ADA, FMLA, ERISA
  - Federal criminal prosecutions

**Diversity Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. § 1332):**
- Parties from different states
- Amount in controversy exceeds **$75,000**
- Complete diversity required

**Supplemental Jurisdiction (28 U.S.C. § 1367):**
- State law claims so related to federal claims they form same case or controversy

### Filing a Federal Case

1. Civil Cover Sheet (JS-44)
2. Complaint — filed via CM/ECF
3. Pay filing fee (currently **$405** for civil cases)
4. Summons issued
5. Serve defendant per FRCP 4
6. FRCP 26(f) conference within 21 days of defendant's appearance
7. Submit scheduling order to magistrate/judge

### Section 1983 Claims — Key Requirements

**Elements (42 U.S.C. § 1983):**
1. Person acting under color of state law
2. Deprived plaintiff of rights, privileges, or immunities secured by Constitution or federal law

**Common § 1983 Claims:**
- Fourth Amendment malicious prosecution (Fox v DeSoto, 6th Cir)
- Fourth Amendment excessive force
- Fourteenth Amendment substantive due process
- First Amendment retaliation
- Equal protection violations

**Qualified Immunity Defense:**
- Defendant not liable unless violated *clearly established* right
- Two-step: (1) Was right violated? (2) Was right clearly established?
- *Pearson v Callahan*, 555 US 223 (2009) — courts may address steps in either order

**Statute of Limitations for § 1983:**
- Borrowed from state personal injury statute
- In Michigan: **3 years** (MCL 600.5805(2))
- Accrual: when plaintiff knew or should have known of injury and its cause

## Part IV — U.S. Court of Appeals — Sixth Circuit

### Filing a Notice of Appeal

- **Civil cases:** within **30 days** of final judgment (FRAP 4(a)(1)(A))
  - Government as a party: within **60 days**
- **Criminal cases:** within **14 days** of judgment (FRAP 4(b)(1)(A))
- Notice filed in district court, not Sixth Circuit

### Briefing Schedule — Sixth Circuit

| Brief | Deadline |
|---|---|
| Appellant's brief | 40 days after record filed in 6th Cir |
| Appellee's brief | 30 days after appellant's brief served |
| Reply brief | 21 days after appellee's brief served |

### Standards of Review in Sixth Circuit

| Issue | Standard |
|---|---|
| Questions of law | De novo |
| Constitutional questions | De novo |
| Factual findings (bench trial) | Clear error |
| Jury verdict | Whether reasonable jury could find for verdict |
| District court discretionary rulings | Abuse of discretion |
| Summary judgment | De novo |
| Sentencing (federal) | Reasonableness / abuse of discretion |

### Sixth Circuit — Special Procedures

- **Mediation program** — automatic screening; most cases referred
- **Oral argument** — presumption of argument unless case is clear
- **En banc review** — petition within 45 days (civil) or 14 days (criminal)
- **Unpublished opinions** — may be cited but not binding (*FRAP 32.1*)

### Certificate of Appealability (Habeas Cases)

- Required for § 2254 and § 2255 appeals
- Must show "substantial showing of denial of constitutional right"
- Apply to district court first; if denied, apply to Sixth Circuit
- *Miller-El v Cockrell*, 537 US 322 (2003) — liberal standard for COA issuance

## Part V — U.S. Supreme Court

### Petition for Certiorari

**When to File:**
- After Sixth Circuit denies rehearing en banc or issues final judgment
- Deadline: **90 days** from entry of judgment or denial of rehearing

**Filing Requirements (Supreme Court Rule 14):**
- 40 pages maximum for petition
- Must include:
  - Questions presented (lead with this — it is the most important page)
  - Table of contents
  - Table of authorities
  - Opinions below (COA opinion and district court opinion)
  - Jurisdiction statement
  - Constitutional/statutory provisions involved
  - Statement of the case (concise)
  - Reasons for granting certiorari
  - Conclusion (precise relief sought)
  - Appendix (opinions of lower courts)

**Filing Fee:** $300
**In Forma Pauperis:** Waiver available (Rule 39)

### Grounds for Certiorari (Supreme Court Rule 10)

> "A petition for a writ of certiorari is rarely granted when the asserted error consists of erroneous factual findings or the misapplication of a properly stated rule of law."

Strong grounds include:
- **Circuit split** — two or more circuits disagree on a federal question
- **State court and federal court conflict** — state court of last resort decided federal question contrary to Supreme Court decisions
- **Significant federal question** not yet settled by Supreme Court
- **Supreme Court precedent directly applicable** and court below departed from it

### After Certiorari is Granted

1. Joint Appendix — compiled by parties (includes key portions of record)
2. Opening brief on the merits — **50 pages**
3. Response brief — **50 pages**
4. Reply brief — **20 pages**
5. Oral argument — **30 minutes per side** (rarely extended)
6. Decision — opinion binding on all courts in U.S.

### Pro Se Petitioners

- May file under Rule 39 (in forma pauperis)
- "Paid" petitions: formal booklet format required (6⅛ × 9¼, saddle-stitched, specific cover colors)
- IFP petitions: 8½ × 11 paper format acceptable
- Clerk of Supreme Court will not accept non-compliant petitions

### Certiorari Grant Rate

- Approximately **1–2%** of petitions granted
- Court receives ~8,000 petitions per term
- Grants ~70–80 cases per term (about 1%)

## Part VI — Key Strategic Principles

### Preservation Is Everything

- Every appellate argument must have been raised and ruled on below
- Failure to preserve = waiver (unless plain error)
- Always make a clear record: "I object on the grounds that..."
- File written objections to referee recommendations (MCR 3.209(E))
- File objections to magistrate's R&R (LCivR 72.2)

### Identify the Right Standard of Review

- De novo review (no deference to trial court) — best for appellant
- Abuse of discretion review — very deferential; hard to win
- Frame every issue to get de novo review if possible
- "The court misapplied the law" → de novo
- "The court made wrong factual finding" → clear error (harder to win)

### Building an Appellate Record

- **Trial counsel must think like an appellate lawyer**
- Create a clear record of every ruling you want reviewed
- File written motions even if you expect to lose
- Obtain written orders on all rulings
- Ensure transcript is accurate; order corrections promptly

### Writing Persuasively

- Courts decide cases based on facts more often than lawyers realize — tell a compelling story
- Cite directly to the record on every factual assertion
- Lead with your strongest argument
- State the rule clearly before applying it
- Be honest about weaknesses — address them; do not hide them
- Write for a judge who knows nothing about your case
- Edit ruthlessly: shorter briefs that make the point clearly are more effective

### Using Unpublished Opinions

- Michigan COA unpublished opinions: not binding but may be cited for persuasive value (MCR 7.215(J)(1))
- Sixth Circuit unpublished: may be cited since 2007 (FRAP 32.1)
- U.S. Supreme Court: all opinions published and binding

### Handling Adverse Controlling Precedent

- Distinguish on facts (most common and effective)
- Argue ruling was limited to specific circumstances
- Argue controlling precedent has been implicitly overruled or undermined
- Ask court to reconsider — frame as invitation to revisit rather than direct attack
- Preserve issue for U.S. Supreme Court certiorari

## Part VII — Checklist for Appellants

### Pre-Filing
- [ ] Identify all issues for appeal
- [ ] Confirm all issues were preserved below
- [ ] Identify applicable standard of review for each issue
- [ ] Calculate and calendar all deadlines
- [ ] Order transcript (do this immediately)
- [ ] Research all controlling and persuasive authority

### Briefing
- [ ] Craft compelling questions presented
- [ ] Write accurate, record-supported statement of facts
- [ ] Write argument section with clear rule statements and applications
- [ ] Address adverse authority
- [ ] Stay within page/word limits
- [ ] Comply with all formatting requirements
- [ ] Include certificate of service and compliance

### Oral Argument Preparation
- [ ] Know the record cold — every fact, every page
- [ ] Prepare for hard questions — the most uncomfortable questions are the ones you must answer
- [ ] Practice answering questions in the middle of your argument (judges will interrupt)
- [ ] Prepare one-sentence answers to key record questions
- [ ] Know your strongest three points; be able to deliver them in any order
- [ ] Know the relief you want — state it clearly if asked
